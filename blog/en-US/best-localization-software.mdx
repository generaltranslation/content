## The localization stack is broken in half

Shipping a multilingual app in 2026 requires stitching together two completely separate systems:

1. **An i18n library** — something that handles string extraction, interpolation, pluralization, and locale routing inside your codebase. You've probably used one. They're good at what they do.
2. **A translation management system (TMS)** — a platform that stores translation files, coordinates translators, and (sometimes) plugs into your CI pipeline.

The library knows nothing about your translations. The TMS knows nothing about your code. **You** are the integration layer: exporting JSON, uploading it, waiting for translators, downloading it, committing it, resolving merge conflicts, and hoping nothing broke.

That's the status quo every "best of" list takes for granted. None of them question it.

## What's wrong with traditional TMS platforms

Most translation management systems were built for a pre-AI, pre-developer-tools world. They assume a specific workflow: content gets extracted from your codebase, uploaded to a dashboard, assigned to translators, reviewed, approved, exported, and re-integrated. It works. It's also slow, expensive, and fragile.

Here's what that model gets wrong:

**They manage files, not code.** A TMS ingests your translation files (JSON, XLIFF, PO, etc.) and gives you tools to manage them. But the real source of truth is your codebase — your components, your variables, your UI hierarchy. Managing the *files* without understanding the *code* means context is always lost in transit.

**Their AI is bolted on, not built in.** Most platforms have added AI/MT features in the last few years, usually by routing strings through third-party machine translation engines and letting you pick which one to use. The AI sees isolated strings. It doesn't see your component tree, your variable names, or the surrounding UI. That's why MT output still needs heavy editing.

**They scale by adding people, not by removing work.** The TMS business model is built around coordinating human translators — roles, permissions, review chains, approval workflows. For enterprise content teams with regulatory requirements, that's necessary. For a dev team shipping a SaaS product in 20 languages, it's overhead that shouldn't exist.

**Integration is always a second-class citizen.** Even the best TMS platforms treat developer integrations as connectors — plugins that sync files between your repo and their dashboard. The sync is never seamless. Merge conflicts happen. Webhooks fail. And every integration is another thing to maintain.

**Pricing is opaque.** Several of the most popular platforms require a sales conversation to get pricing. Others charge per-seat fees that balloon as your team grows. When you can't predict what localization will cost, it's hard to justify expanding to new markets.

## What's wrong with standalone i18n libraries

On the other side, popular i18n libraries for React and Next.js are excellent at what they do: rendering translated content inside your app. They handle interpolation, pluralization, locale routing, and Server Component support.

**But they don't translate anything.**

They're rendering engines for translations you produce elsewhere. You still need to:
- Extract every translatable string into JSON files
- Maintain a separate file for every locale
- Either translate those files manually, hire translators, or plug in a TMS
- Keep every file in sync as your codebase changes

At 10 languages and 500 keys, that's 5,000 entries to maintain. At 50 languages it's 25,000. The library doesn't help you with any of that — it just renders whatever you give it.

The best libraries in the ecosystem are lightweight, well-typed, and performant. They just solve half the problem.

```tsx
      placeholder={gt('Enter your email')}

  );

```

## Context-aware AI, not generic MT

Most localization AI works like this: take a string, translate it in isolation, return the result. "Apple" could be the fruit or the company. "Cell" could be biological or a spreadsheet. Without context, the AI guesses.

General Translation's AI sees your full codebase context — component hierarchy, variable names, surrounding UI, your product glossary. It produces translations that are idiomatic from the first draft, not after three rounds of editing.

You can also add explicit context:

```tsx
<T context="the technology company">Apple</T>
```

The result: fewer review cycles, faster time to production, and translations that actually sound like your product — not like they were run through a generic translation API.
